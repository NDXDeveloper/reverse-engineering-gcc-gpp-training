> 📁 **Part IX — Resources & Automation**

## Why this chapter exists

Reverse engineering is a discipline that can't be learned solely from a tutorial. Tools evolve, protections grow more complex, formats change, and new architectures appear regularly. What you've acquired over the previous 35 chapters constitutes a solid foundation — but that's precisely what it is: a foundation.

Progression in RE rests on three complementary pillars:

1. **Regular practice** — Analyzing varied binaries, in different contexts, with time or knowledge constraints. CTF competitions and training platforms are the ideal terrain for this.

2. **Theoretical deepening** — Understanding processor architectures, binary format specifications, compiler implementation details, or operating systems. This involves reading reference books, academic papers, and technical blogs maintained by practitioners.

3. **Community exchange** — RE is a domain where you progress enormously through contact with other analysts. Conferences, specialized forums, and write-ups shared after CTFs are an invaluable source of learning. You discover approaches you would never have thought of on your own.

This chapter gathers the resources we consider most relevant for continuing your progression after this training. It's not an exhaustive list — the field is too vast for that — but a targeted selection, organized by resource type, with for each an indication of the required level and what it concretely provides.

## How to leverage these resources effectively

**Don't try to do everything at once.** The temptation is great to sign up on five platforms, buy three books, and join ten Discord servers on the same day. The usual result is not deepening anything.

A more productive approach is to choose **one practice platform** as a regular anchor, **one book** to read alongside your analysis sessions, and **one community** where you share your results and ask questions. Then adjust over time, based on the areas you want to deepen.

RE is a marathon, not a sprint. Consistency in practice — even one hour per week on a crackme or challenge — produces results far superior to intensive sessions spaced months apart.
